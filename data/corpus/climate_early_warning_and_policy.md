# Climate Migration Early Warning Systems and Policy Responses

## Data for climate migration early warning systems

Climate migration early warning systems combine several categories of
origin-side data: crop failure data (agricultural yield shortfalls
relative to historical baselines), drought indices (standardized measures
of precipitation deficit and soil moisture over time), and flood frequency
records for the origin region. On their own, these physical indicators
only describe environmental stress, not migration - to turn them into a
usable forecast, they need to be paired with historical migration response
lag, meaning the typically observed delay between a climate shock and the
resulting rise in out-migration from that region (this lag varies by
region and by the type of shock, but is rarely instantaneous). Finally,
combining this origin-side picture with receiving-city capacity data
(housing vacancy, job growth, public service utilization - see
`receiving_city_capacity.md`) is what allows an early warning system to
move from "a climate shock is occurring" to "this specific corridor and
receiving city are likely to see a capacity-relevant inflow within a
specific window."

## Policy responses that improve integration outcomes

Cities that plan proactively, rather than reactively, show measurably
better integration outcomes when facing climate-driven migration. Three
concrete proactive measures stand out in comparative case studies:

1. **Pre-zoning land for rapid housing expansion** - identifying and
   pre-approving sites for fast-track housing construction before an
   inflow occurs, rather than starting the zoning and approval process
   only once housing pressure is already acute.
2. **Building modular, expandable school capacity** - designing school
   infrastructure that can add classroom capacity incrementally, instead
   of relying on fixed-capacity buildings that hit a hard ceiling.
3. **Establishing simplified registration processes for new arrivals** -
   reducing the administrative friction for new residents to access
   housing, schooling, and healthcare, which shortens the window during
   which arrivals are effectively invisible to city services and
   therefore under-served.

Cities that wait until strain is already visible - overcrowded schools,
housing shortages, informal settlements - consistently show worse
integration outcomes than cities that treat migration capacity planning as
an ongoing exercise rather than a crisis response.
