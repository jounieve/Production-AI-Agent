# Push and Pull Factors in Migration Destination Choice

## Social networks as a pull factor

Social networks matter enormously in migration destination choice.
Migrants disproportionately move to cities where family members or people
from their home region have already settled, because these networks lower
the cost of arrival in three concrete ways: they provide housing leads
(a place to stay while searching, or direct knowledge of affordable
listings), job referrals (informal hiring networks are often faster and
more accessible than formal job markets for new arrivals), and language
support (translation, help navigating bureaucracy, cultural orientation).
This means existing diaspora concentration is often a stronger predictor
of destination choice than a city's raw economic indicators alone — a
city with mediocre wages but a large existing community from the same
origin region can out-compete a city with better wages but no existing
network.

## Economic push factors

The main economic push factors driving migration are chronic unemployment,
wage stagnation, and the collapse of local industries. Chronic
unemployment removes the primary reason to stay; wage stagnation erodes
purchasing power even for those who remain employed, especially when
compared to rising costs of living; and the collapse of a dominant local
industry (a single major employer, an agricultural sector hit by falling
commodity prices, a manufacturing sector undercut by external competition)
can remove the economic base of an entire region at once, producing a
sudden and geographically concentrated push effect rather than a gradual
one.

## Climate displacement as a dominant push factor

Climate-related displacement is considered an increasingly dominant push
factor because prolonged drought, repeated flooding, and sea-level rise in
coastal regions destroy agricultural livelihoods directly and force
affected households to seek income elsewhere. Unlike a single acute shock
(one bad harvest, one flood), these climate mechanisms tend to be
recurring and worsening over successive years, which converts what might
once have been a temporary, reversible setback into a structural,
long-term reason to leave — this is what distinguishes climate
displacement from ordinary economic hardship in push-factor analysis.

## Why climate displacement funnels into cities rather than other rural areas

Climate displacement increasingly funnels into urban areas rather than
into other rural regions because cities offer wage labor opportunities
that rural areas hit by the same climate shock generally cannot provide.
When a drought or flood affects an agricultural region, neighboring rural
areas are frequently exposed to correlated climate risk and offer little
additional labor demand for displaced agricultural workers. Urban labor
markets, by contrast, are largely decoupled from local agricultural
conditions and can absorb workers into construction, services, and
informal-sector work regardless of the originating region's climate
shock — which is why the receiving end of a climate migration corridor is
overwhelmingly a city rather than a neighboring rural area.
